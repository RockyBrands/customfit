Understanding Your
Footwear Options

Electrical hazard (EH) rated safety footwear and dielectric footwear/overshoes help to protect employees from electrical shock and other hazards in the workplace. Read more below for some of the key differences so you can make an informed decision on the right option for your workforce.

Different Electrical Footwear Types

EH / Dielectric
Reduces electrical flow through body

Static Dissipative (SD)
Controls static discharge

Conductive
Allows static to pass safely to ground

Electrical Hazard (EH) Rated Safety Footwear

Footwear certified under ASTM International F2413 with an EH (Electrical Hazard) rating is tested to withstand:

  • 18,000 volts at 60 Hz for 1 minute
  • Limited leakage current during testing
  • Non-conductive soles and heels
  • No exposed metal components

These boots are designed to reduce the risk of completing an electrical circuit if accidental contact occurs.

Typical features

  • Leather uppers (synthetics may melt in arc flash)
  • Composite toe (often preferred for electrical work)
  • Slip-resistant and oil-resistant outsoles
  • 6-8 inch ankle support
  • No mesh panels or meltable fabrics

Standard athletic shoes, canvas footwear, or mesh sneakers are not acceptable for arc flash work environments.

Arc-Rated (AR) / Flame-resistant (FR) Considerations

In higher hazard categories under NFPA 70E, footwear must also resist heat and ignition. Common requirements include:

  • Full leather construction
  • Heat-resistant rubber or nitrile soles
  • Stitching that resists ignition
  • No exposed meltable materials

Choosing the right option for your workforce

Every environment is different. The correct footwear depends on:

  • Voltage exposure levels
  • Arc flash hazard category
  • Work tasks performed
  • Environmental conditions
  • Company safety policy and more...

Care & Inspection of Footwear Matters

Electrical protection depends heavily on condition. Avoid footwear with:

  • Cracked or worn soles
  • Embedded nails or metal fragments
  • Wet or contaminated materials
  • Sole modifications

If insulation integrity is questionable, replacement is critical.
